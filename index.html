¿Qué está pasando?

(What's going on?)

The Haitian - Dominican Border Situation

By Rosalía Jiménez

The 2015 Plan for the Regulatization of Foreigners in the Dominican Republic (DR) is an initiative launched in the DR, under President Danilo Medina, created to legitimate undocumented people's immigration status in the country by allowing these immigrants apply for a legal status in DR.

Even though it had a lot of positive outcomes, this also provoked the returnement of not less than 250,000 Haitian and Dominicans of Haitian descent labouring in DR to Haiti bewtween June 2015 and March 2018. Many of the deportations that came from this regulation plan "did not meet international standards" since many of them left under pressure or even threat. Consequently, this left people in a non-notice limbo of erratic deportations, creating a lot of public argument and general disagreement.

After the ending of th Plan for the Regularization of Foreigners in 2018, over 200,000 Haitians stayed in the DR without legal paperwork, risking themselves to be deported.

Today, Haiti not only faces economic, political and social instability, it's also dealing with many environmental issues that bring within other situations like poor sanitation and food as a result of not having enough natural resources + bad management of goods; But this doesn't mean that haitians are any safer in the DR. The deportation and irregular immigration statuses of haitians caused a congested illicit trade between Haiti and Dominican Republic in the border.

Black haitian people protesting in a straight horizontal line
in the Dominican streets. Most of them have white signs with
messages, one of them is slightly burnt. Another protester closer to
our view, is holding a sign with their hands as they show the message written on it “I have rights just like you” written in red and blue letters (which are the colors of both Dominican and Haitian flags).
This person also has their mouth covered with a blue strip of cloth.

Tired and sweaty, Black Haitian people are crossing barefoot
a polluted river called Massacre which divides Haiti and Dominican Republic, while carrying goods they intend to trade illegally with
the other side of the river. The place has plenty of rocks in the
ground and it seems to be the afternoon. In the background there’s
a group of people in line, also waiting to negotiate with the Dominican merchants behind the bridge constructed below the river to cross it.  

This is the Haitian - Dominican border from an upper view
high enough to see both side’s hills and nature. The left side
is the Haitian side, which seems to have very few trees and
forests to cover its ground. Different from Haiti’s side, the
opposite Dominican side is covered in forests. The border
abruptly divides both countries.

Group of black children standing barefoot in the ground.

colors. There is a dirty concrete wall with broken plants in front
